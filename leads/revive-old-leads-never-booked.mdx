Old leads are not dead, most are just unanswered. A short, well-timed re-engagement message, sent by text or email with a single clear ask, can turn a chunk of a stale contact list back into booked appointments without spending another dollar on ads.

## What does it mean to "revive" an old lead?

Lead revival is defined as the deliberate act of re-contacting someone who inquired in the past but never booked, converted, or explicitly opted out. It is different from cold outreach because the person already raised their hand once. A reactivation campaign means sending that group a new message designed to restart the conversation, not a repeat of the original pitch that already failed to land.

The list itself usually already exists. Every home service business with more than a year of history has one: names sitting in a CRM tagged "no response," "left voicemail," or simply untouched since the form came in.

## Why do leads go cold before they book?

Most leads do not go cold because they lost interest. They go cold because nobody followed up fast enough or often enough. A Harvard Business Review study by Oldroyd and McElheran found that companies contacting a lead within an hour were roughly seven times more likely to have a meaningful conversation with that lead than companies that waited even a little longer. Once that early window closes, momentum is gone and the lead sits.

A few other common reasons leads never book:

- The first response came a day or two late, after the homeowner already called a competitor.
- Follow-up stopped after one or two attempts instead of continuing on a schedule.
- The original message assumed urgency the homeowner did not feel yet (a roof replacement, a kitchen remodel) and nobody checked back in later.
- Pricing or scheduling friction killed the conversation and nobody addressed it afterward.
- The lead was mid-research, comparing quotes, and the business never resurfaced when the decision window reopened.

None of these mean the person is unreachable. They mean the timing or the message was wrong the first time.

## How do you build a re-engagement campaign that actually works?

Start with the list, not the message. Pull every contact with no completed appointment and no explicit "stop contacting me," segmented by how old the lead is and what they originally asked about. A lead from three weeks ago gets a different message than one from fourteen months ago.

From there, the sequence generally looks like this:

1. Segment the list by lead age and original service interest.
2. Send a short opener that references the original inquiry without over-explaining it.
3. Space follow-ups out over one to two weeks rather than firing everything on day one.
4. Offer one easy next step: reply "yes," click a booking link, or answer a single qualifying question.
5. Stop the sequence the moment someone books, replies negatively, or opts out.

Volume matters less than sequencing. A four-message sequence spread over two weeks consistently outperforms a single blast, because it catches people at different points in their own timeline, not just yours.

## What should a revival message actually say?

Keep it short, specific, and low-pressure. A re-engagement text works best when it acknowledges time has passed, references what the person originally asked about, and asks one plain question. Something like: "Hi {{contact.first_name}}, following up on the estimate you asked about a while back, still something you're looking to get done this year?"

Avoid re-pitching the whole service in the first message. The goal of message one is just a reply, not a booking. Once someone responds, that is when qualifying questions and scheduling links belong in the conversation.

Email can carry a bit more context than SMS, since it is not competing for the same immediate attention, but the same rule applies: one ask, one link, no wall of text.

## DIY re-engagement vs AI-run reactivation: what's the real difference?

| | Manual / DIY | AI-run reactivation (Ares) |
|---|---|---|
| Segmenting the old lead list | Manual export and filtering | Automatic, by age and original inquiry |

Neither approach requires new ad spend. The difference is mostly consistency: automation does not forget to send message three, and it does not let a reply sit in an inbox overnight.

## A hypothetical example: a fencing company's dead lead list

This is an illustration, not a claimed client result. Say a residential fencing company has around four hundred contacts sitting in its CRM going back eighteen months, all originally form-fills or missed calls that never turned into an estimate. Nobody has touched most of them since the week they came in.

If that list gets segmented by age and original project type, and a four-message SMS and email sequence goes out over two weeks referencing each person's original inquiry, a real share of that list typically replies. Some are still shopping for a fence and never picked anyone. Some moved, or already had the work done, and say so, which at least clears the list. The ones who reply interested get qualified and booked automatically, and the ones who need a real conversation get flagged to the owner instead of sitting untouched for another eighteen months.

## Frequently asked questions

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="How long after a lead goes cold is it worth trying to revive it?">
There is no hard cutoff. Leads from the last few weeks tend to respond best because the original need is often still active, but contacts twelve or more months old still convert a share of the time, especially in services people delay, like remodels or replacements.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Will re-engaging old leads annoy people or hurt my reputation?">
A single well-worded, low-pressure message rarely does. The risk comes from repeated messages after someone has ignored or declined once. Any reactivation sequence should stop automatically the moment someone opts out or replies negatively.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Should I text or email old leads first?">
Text generally gets read and replied to faster, which makes it a strong first touch. Email works well as a second channel or for leads where you never captured a phone number. Running both in the same sequence usually outperforms either alone.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Do I need new ad spend to revive old leads?">
No. These are contacts already acquired, so reactivation is close to free compared with generating new leads through [ad campaigns](/ads/campaigns). It is often the highest-return item on a home service business's list precisely because there is no new spend involved.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Can this be automated instead of done manually every time?">

</Accordion>
